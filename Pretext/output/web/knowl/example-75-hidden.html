The standard basis
\begin{equation*} \vec{e}_1, \vec{e}_2, \ldots, \vec{e}_n\text{,} \end{equation*}
is a basis of \({\mathbb R}^n\text{,}\) (hence the name). So as expected
\begin{equation*} \dim {\mathbb R}^n = n\text{.} \end{equation*}
On the other hand the subspace \(\{ \vec{0} \}\) is of dimension \(0\text{.}\)
The subspace \(S''\) from a previous example, that is, the set of vectors \((a,a)\) is of dimensionÂ 1. One possible basis is simply \(\{ (1,1) \}\text{,}\) the single vector \((1,1)\text{:}\) every vector in \(S''\) can be represented by \(a (1,1) = (a,a)\text{.}\) Similarly another possible basis would be \(\{ (-1,-1) \}\text{.}\) Then the vector \((a,a)\) would be represented as \((-a) (1,1)\text{.}\)
