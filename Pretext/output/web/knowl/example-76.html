Example 6.4.4.

In the last section, we considered the matrix
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \\ 2 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \\ 3 \amp 6 \amp 7 \amp 8 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Using row reduction to find the pivot columns, we found
\begin{equation*} \text{ column space of \(A\) } \left( \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \\ 2 \amp 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \\ 3 \amp 6 \amp 7 \amp 8 \end{bmatrix} \right) = \operatorname{span} \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} , \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \\ 7 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
What we did was we found the basis of the column space. The basis has two elements, and so the column space of \(A\) is two dimensional. Notice that the rank of \(A\) is two.
